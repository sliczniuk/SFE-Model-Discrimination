	Mathematical models are constructed based on specific hypotheses regarding physical phenomena and are subsequently validated using experimental data. Each model predicts the response $y$ as a function of experimental conditions $\Xi$ and model parameters $\theta$ (parameters associated with extraction kinetics) and constitutes a subset of the parameter space $\Theta$. Although the models differ in their structures and parameter sets, certain parameters may retain consistent physical interpretations across models.
	
	The Appendix \ref{chap:Parameter_Estimation_Challanger} introduces an alternative formulation to the model described in Section \ref{chap:model}, specifically replacing the extraction kinetic term from Section \ref{CH: Kinetic}. When multiple models are available, selecting the final model is a critical decision. A straightforward approach is to select the model with the lowest modelling error; however, this method is inadequate when several models exhibit similar performance. Alternatively, the relative evidence supporting one model over another can be evaluated. If such comparisons remain inconclusive, a new experiment should be designed at the operating conditions where the model outputs differ most significantly. 
	
	The single-point design of experiment for model discrimination, as presented in Section \ref{sec:SinglePointMD}, can be extended to accommodate multiple data points. In this approach, the system is operated such that the model outputs exhibit maximal statistical divergence, based on information obtained from prior experiments. The criterion for selecting subsequent experiments incorporates posterior probabilities as weighting factors. Following \citet{Himmelblau1970}, the objective function is defined as:
	
	{\footnotesize
		\begin{equation}
			K_v = \tfrac{1}{2} \, P_1^{(n)} \, P_2^{(n)} \cdot j_{DJ}
			\label{eq:Kv}
	\end{equation}}
	
	Here, $j_{DJ}$ denotes the Jeffreys divergence between the predictive distributions of the two models. The term $P_1^{(n)} P_2^{(n)}$ weights the design according to the current posterior model probabilities, as described in Section \ref{sec:Bayesian_Factor}. When one model has been effectively eliminated, $K_v$ remains small under all experimental conditions.
	
	Both models assume normally distributed errors, with predicted covariance matrices $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_2$, respectively (see Appendix \ref{App:Cov}):
	
	{\footnotesize 
		\begin{equation} p_i(y|\Xi) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^{n_Y} |\Sigma_i|}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} (Y(\Xi) - y_i(\Xi))^\top \Sigma_i^{-1} (Y(\Xi) - y_i(\Xi)) \right) 
	\end{equation} }
	
	Here, $Y(\Xi)$ denotes the observed data, $y_i(\Xi)$ represents the predicted response from model $i$, and $\Xi$ indicates the operating conditions. The model parameters are fixed at $\hat{\theta}$, so $p_i(y|\Xi, \hat{\theta})$ is abbreviated as $p_i(y|\Xi)$.
	
	Prior to conducting a subsequent experiment, the output $Y(\Xi)$ remains unknown, which precludes direct computation of $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$. Assuming Model~1 is correct and the observations are normally distributed, a closed-form expression for $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ divergence can be derived as follows:
	
	{\footnotesize \begin{equation} 
			D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_1(y|\Xi) \| p_2(y|\Xi) \right) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \ln\left( \frac{|\Sigma_2|}{|\Sigma_1|} \right) - n_Y + \mathrm{tr}\left( \Sigma_2^{-1} \Sigma_1 \right) + (y_2(\Xi) - y_1(\Xi))^\top \Sigma_2^{-1} (y_2(\Xi) - y_1(\Xi)) \right] 
	\end{equation} }
	
	Analogously, $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_2(y|\Xi) \| p_1(y|\Xi) \right)$ can be obtained by interchanging the indices. Because the correct model is unknown, both quantities are combined to define the objective function $j_{Dj}$. This symmetric combination of two KL divergences is referred to as the Jeffreys divergence:
	
	{\footnotesize 
		\begin{equation} 
			j_{Dj} = D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_1(y|\Xi) \| p_2(y|\Xi) \right) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_2(y|\Xi) \| p_1(y|\Xi) \right) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \left[ p_1(y|\Xi) - p_2(y|\Xi) \right] \ln\left( \frac{p_1(y|\Xi)}{p_2(y|\Xi)} \right) dy 
	\end{equation} }
	
	A large value of $j_{DJ}$ is obtained when $p_2(y|\Xi)$ differs substantially from $p_1(y|\Xi)$, or vice versa. In either case, the outcome indicates a strong preference for one model over the other. By expanding the objective function, its algebraic form can be obtained:
	
	{\footnotesize 
		\begin{equation} 
			j_{DJ} = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \mathrm{tr}\left( \Sigma_1^{-1} \Sigma_2 + \Sigma_2^{-1} \Sigma_1 \right) - 2n_Y + (y_2(\Xi) - y_1(\Xi))^\top \left( \Sigma_1^{-1} + \Sigma_2^{-1} \right) (y_2(\Xi) - y_1(\Xi)) \right] 
	\end{equation} }
	
	Maximising $j_{DJ}$ encourages operating conditions in which the predictive distributions of competing models are maximally separated, given the associated uncertainties. Experimental data generated under the true model are expected to yield a lower likelihood when assessed under the alternative hypothesis. Owing to the symmetry of the Jeffreys divergence, the solution to the optimisation problem suggests that one of the two hypotheses is correct. This method assumes that one of the models will accurately predict the data from the subsequent experiment.
	
	
	\subsubsection{Problem formulation}
	
	The optimisation problem (Equation \ref{EQ:Formulation}) is addressed by varying temperature, mass flow rate, and pressure. Data are assumed to be collected cumulatively, adhering to the sampling intervals established in the initial experiments. Decision variables are normalised between -1 and 1, with penalty weights set to 0.001. The inlet temperature and mass flow rate are adjusted every 10 min, whereas the pressure is modified every 30 min. The parameter $\tau_P$ is set to 120 sec to balance system dynamics and the smoothness of pressure derivatives. Notably, system pressure reaches the step-change value after 10 min, resulting in the overlap between piece-wise control intervals and system pressure for most of the experiment.
	
	{\footnotesize
		\begin{equation}
			\begin{aligned}
				\Xi^* &= \arg \min_{T^{in}(t),\,F(t), P(t)}
				-\Big(j_{DJ} + j_{\mathrm{smooth}}\Big) \\
				\text{subject to} \qquad
				\dot{x}(t) &= G\big(x(t),t,\Theta;\Xi\big), \\
				t_0 &= 0~\mathrm{min}, \\
				t_f &= 600~\mathrm{min}, \\
				%| F_{k+1}-F_{k} | &\leq 50 ~\mathrm{bar}, \\
				30~\mathrm{^\circ C} \leq T^{in}(t) &\leq 40~\mathrm{^\circ C}, \\
				3.3 \times 10^{-5} \leq F(t) &\leq 6.7 \times 10^{-5}, \\
				100 \leq P(t) &\leq 200 \mathrm{bar}, \\
				j_{\mathrm{smooth}} = 
				\alpha_T \sum_{k=1}^{N_t-1}\left(T^{in}_{k+1}-T^{in}_{k}\right)^2
				&+
				\alpha_F \sum_{k=1}^{N_t-1}\left(F_{k+1}-F_{k}\right)^2
				+
				\alpha_P \sum_{k=1}^{N_t-1}\left(P_{k+1}-P_{k}\right)^2
			\end{aligned}
			\label{EQ:Formulation}
	\end{equation}}
	
	\subsubsection{Numerical results}
	
	Figure \ref{fig:MD_Opt_Traj_scatter} compares the initial and final values of the cost function. Most results reached an objective function value of 45.5, indicating convergence to a global solution. The optimal control profiles (Figures~\ref{fig:MD_Opt_Traj_T}--\ref{fig:MD_Opt_Traj_F}) exhibit a two-phase structure. During the first phase, from 0 to 200 min, the inlet temperature remains nearly constant at their initial values, while the pressure rises. In the second phase (200 min onward), the inlet temperature increases gradually, while pressure stays at the same level. The mass flow rate is kept at its upper bound during the entire batch.
	
	Figure~\ref{fig:MD_Opt_Traj_Y} shows that, initially, the predicted yield curves from both models follow similar trajectories but diverge over time. The Jeffreys divergence objective maximises the statistical separation of the output distributions over entire trajectories, rather than pointwise differences. Analysis of the output distributions at the final time reveals an overlap (Figure \ref{fig:MD_Opt_Traj_dist_2}), which warrants further investigation. Table \ref{tab:Opt_trj_disc_meas} presents the discrimination measures evaluated at the final time. In the absence of measurement error, the optimised trajectory achieves perfect separation (AUC = 1.0, KS = 1.0). When empirical measurement noise is introduced, the AUC decreases to 0.89 and the Jensen--Shannon divergence to 0.27. These values indicate only a modest improvement over the best constant-condition scenario (Figure~\ref{fig:Discrimination_index_666_with_modelling_error}). The screening maps in Figures~\ref{fig:AUC_scatter} and~\ref{fig:JS_scatter} clarify this result: the high-discrimination region at elevated pressure and flow rate is broad and relatively flat, so constant conditions already capture most of the discrimination capability.

	Interpretation of the results should consider that the objective function presumes one of the two models accurately represents the data from the subsequent experiment. If both models are inadequate, the designed experiment may only achieve high discrimination between two incorrect alternatives. In such a scenario, a subsequent experiment conducted according to the derived procedure $\Xi^*$ would not correspond to any of the predicted yield curves. This outcome would indicate insufficient modelling assumptions and advocate for the model redevelopment.
