\section{Model discrimination} \label{chap:DOE_discrimination}

Mathematical models are constructed based on specific hypotheses regarding physical phenomena and are subsequently validated using experimental data. Each model predicts the response $y$ as a function of experimental conditions $\Xi$ and model parameters $\theta$ (parameters associated with extraction kinetics) and constitutes a subset of the parameter space $\Theta$. Although the models differ in their structures and parameter sets, certain parameters may retain consistent physical interpretations across models.

The Appendix \ref{chap:Parameter_Estimation_Challanger} introduces an alternative formulation to the model described in Section \ref{chap:model}, specifically replacing the extraction kinetic term from Section \ref{CH: Kinetic}. When multiple models are available, selecting the final model is a critical decision. A straightforward approach is to select the model with the lowest modelling error; however, this method is inadequate when several models exhibit similar performance. Alternatively, the relative evidence supporting one model over another can be evaluated. If such comparisons remain inconclusive, a new experiment should be designed at the operating conditions where the model outputs differ most significantly. 

\subsection{Bayesian Factor approach} \label{sec:Bayesian_Factor}

According to the sequential model discrimination procedure described by \citet{Box1967}, the objective of model selection is to identify the model that best represents the data. As noted by \citet{Box1967}, \citet{Kass1995}, \citet{Bishop2008}, \citet{Rasmussen2006}, \citet{Himmelblau1970}, and other researchers, Bayes' theorem provides a quantitative framework for evaluating how well each model accounts for the observed evidence or data. After conducting $n$ experiments, the posterior probability that model $i$ is correct is updated sequentially using Bayes' theorem:

{\footnotesize
	\begin{equation}
		P_i^{(n)} = \frac{P_i^{(n-1)} \, p_i(y^{(n)}(\Xi))}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{N_{Model}} P_k^{(n-1)} \, p_k(y^{(n)}(\Xi))}
		\label{eq:posterior}
\end{equation}}

where $P_i^{(0)} = 1/2$ reflects an uninformative prior, $p_i(y^{(n)}(\Xi))$ is the likelihood of the $n$th observation under model $i$, and $N_{Model}$ is the number of compared models (2 in this work). Under the assumption of Gaussian predictive errors and treating the predictive covariance $\Sigma_i^{(k)}$ (Appendix~\ref{App:Cov}) as incorporating parameter uncertainty through error propagation, the cumulative log-likelihood after all $n$ experiments is:

{\footnotesize
	\begin{equation}
		\ln L_i^{(n)} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \left[ -\frac{1}{2} \ln |\Sigma_i^{(k)}| - \frac{n_Y}{2} \ln(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2} \left( Y^{(k)}(\Xi) - y_i^{(k)}(\Xi) \right)^\top \left(\Sigma_i^{(k)}\right)^{-1} \left( Y^{(k)}(\Xi) - y_i^{(k)}(\Xi) \right) \right]
		\label{eq:loglik}
\end{equation}}

The superscript $(k)$ indicates that the values were obtained from experiment $k$. $\Sigma_i^{(k)}$ denotes the predicted covariance matrix, $Y^{(k)}$ represents the observed data vector, and $y_i^{(k)}$ refers to the predicted response from model $i$. Assuming all models are equally likely prior to observing the data, the posterior model probability can be expressed in terms of the marginal log-likelihoods. Equation \ref{eq:posterior_loglik} is derived directly from Bayes' rule after reparametrisation and presents the likelihood ratio in logarithmic form:

{\footnotesize
	\begin{align}
		P_1^{(n)} &= \frac{1}{1 + \exp  \left(\ln L_2^{(n)} - \ln L_1^{(n)}\right) } \nonumber \\
		P_2^{(n)} &= 1 - P_1^{(n)}
		\label{eq:posterior_loglik}
\end{align}}

When the probability surpasses a predefined threshold (e.g., $P_i^{(n)} > 0.95$), the experiments are considered to provide sufficient information for model selection. Beyond cumulative posterior probabilities (Equation \ref{eq:posterior_loglik}), the evidence from each experiment can be quantified using the per-experiment Bayes Factor. For experiment $k$, the Bayes Factor comparing Model 1 to Model 2 is defined as the ratio of the marginal likelihoods:

{\footnotesize
	\begin{equation}
		\ln BF_{12}^{(k)} = \ln L_1\!\left(y^{(k)}(\Xi)\right) - \ln L_2\!\left(y^{(k)}(\Xi)\right)
\end{equation}}

Values of the Bayes factor greater or smaller than zero indicate evidence favouring one model over the other; this constitutes model selection. The Bayes Factor is the per-experiment tool (discrimination), while the posterior probability is the accumulated result (selection).

The initial two experiments reveal discrepancies in $P_L$ across different model measurements. Specifically, cumulative data initially favour the Power model, while differentiated and normalised measurements provide stronger support for the Linear model. Incorporating further experiments increases the posterior probability above 0.9, irrespective of the model's output formulation. The cumulative posterior probabilities are $P(\mathrm{Linear}) \approx 1$ and $P(\mathrm{Power}) \approx 0$ in each case. Although the Power model contains fewer parameters, it exhibits a slightly higher average mean squared error. The differentiated and normalised yields exhibit stable behaviour, although they diverge over time. After twelve experiments, the BIC method favours the Power model for normalised yield, whereas the results for differentiated yield favour the Linear model. The cumulative yield results display greater variation but ultimately support the Linear model.

\subsection{Single-point-based approach} \label{sec:SinglePointMD}

If the procedure described in Section \ref{sec:Bayesian_Factor} fails to identify a single model that adequately represents the data, additional experiments are required. This section presents a theoretical framework for designing such experiments, emphasising the identification of experimental conditions that maximise the difference between model outputs while accounting for parameter uncertainty and modelling errors.

\citet{Box1967}, \citet{Himmelblau1970}, and \citet{Bard1974} demonstrated that the ratio of two probability distributions serves as a measure of evidence favouring one model over another. The expression $\ln\left( \frac{p_1(y|\Xi)}{p_2(y|\Xi)} \right)$ quantifies the odds in favour of hypothesis $H_1$ (\(p_1(y|\Xi)\) is the true model), relative to $H_2$ (\(p_2(y|\Xi)\) is the true model). For specific operating conditions \(\Xi\), this evidence can be expressed using the Kullback--Leibler divergence \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}\):

{\footnotesize
\begin{equation}
	D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_1(y|\Xi) | p_2(y|\Xi) \right) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p_1(y|\Xi) \ln\left( \frac{p_1(y|\Xi)}{p_2(y|\Xi)} \right) \mathrm{d}y
\end{equation} }

$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_1(y|\Xi) | p_2(y|\Xi))$ quantifies the extent to which data generated from $p_1$ can be distinguished from data generated from $p_2$. A large value suggests that observations from $p_1$ are unlikely to be confused with those from $p_2$. In contrast, $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_2(y|\Xi) | p_1(y|\Xi))$ measures the information loss incurred when $p_1$ is used to approximate $p_2$. These divergences serve as asymmetric metrics of distance between probability distributions. Under $H_1$, it is advantageous to design an experiment $\Xi$ that is likely to yield a large value of $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_1(y|\Xi) | p_2(y|\Xi) \right)$; the converse applies for Model~2.

The probability distributions $p_1(y|\Xi)$ and $p_2(y|\Xi)$ may be derived analytically under Gaussian assumptions or computed numerically using Monte Carlo (MC) methods. In this section, the MC approach is employed to evaluate the predictive probability density function of the output of model $i$ at fixed operating conditions, accounting for parameter uncertainty:

{\footnotesize
\begin{equation} \label{EQ:p_i_d_theta}
	p_i(y(t|\Xi)) = \int p_i(y(t)\mid\theta_i, \Xi) , p_i(\theta_i\mid\hat{\theta}_i,\Sigma_i,\Xi) , \mathrm{d}\theta_i
\end{equation} }

Equation~\ref{EQ:p_i_d_theta} accounts for parameter uncertainty by marginalising over the posterior distribution $p_i(\theta_i \mid \hat{\theta}_i, \Sigma_i, \Xi)$, where $\hat{\theta}i$ and $\Sigma_i$ denote the parameter estimates and covariance, respectively. The integral is approximated using methods such as kernel density estimation (KDE) with a Gaussian kernel. Monte Carlo (MC) simulations generate independent and identically distributed samples of model outputs. KDE is then applied to these samples to estimate the probability density function $p_i(y|\Xi)$. This process is performed for both models, and the resulting densities are used to numerically compute the $D{\mathrm{KL}}$ divergence. A degenerate distribution for $p_i(y|\Xi)$ does not pose a problem in this context because marginalisation over parameter uncertainty provides sufficient variability.

While the $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ divergence quantifies differences between distributions, its asymmetry ($D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P|L) \neq D_{\mathrm{KL}}(L|P)$) and lack of an upper bound complicate direct comparisons of operating conditions. To overcome these limitations, the Jensen–Shannon divergence ($D_J$) is employed. When the logarithm base is 2, $D_J$ is bounded between 0 and 1 for discrete distributions; otherwise, the upper bound is $\ln(2)$.

{\footnotesize
	\begin{align}
		D_J &= \frac{1}{2} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_1(y|\Xi) | p_M(y|\Xi) \right) + \frac{1}{2} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left( p_2(y|\Xi) | p_M(y|\Xi) \right) \nonumber \\
		p_M(y|\Xi) &= \frac{1}{2} \left( p_1(y|\Xi) + p_2(y|\Xi) \right)  
\end{align} }

Additionally, two complementary discrimination diagnostics are employed. The two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) test is a non-parametric method for assessing distributional separation without assuming a specific distributional form. This test assesses whether the output samples from both models are drawn from the same distribution. The KS statistic ranges from 0 (identical distributions) to 1 (no overlap).

The area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve (AUC) is analogous to the performance of binary classification. Samples from each model are assigned to distinct classes, and the AUC quantifies the probability that a randomly selected sample from one model exceeds a randomly selected sample from the other. An AUC of 0.5 indicates no discrimination, meaning the output distributions overlap completely, while values approaching 0 or 1 indicate strong separation.

The distribution of the pointwise differences between the two model outputs can be examined directly. If zero lies outside the 95\% confidence interval of this difference distribution, the models are considered to produce statistically distinguishable predictions under that operating condition.

\subsubsection{Numerical results} 

Figures~\ref{fig:AUC_scatter} and~\ref{fig:JS_scatter} present AUC and $D_J$ values calculated from the cumulative yield at the final time point (600~min) under various operating conditions (four discrete values of P were used, along with a continuous range of T and F, as specified by the original experimental conditions), assuming the absence of measurement noise. At elevated pressures (160 and 200~bar), discrimination potential increases with mass flow rate. At 120~bar, distinguishing between models becomes challenging in regions of high mass flow rate and elevated temperature. At 100~bar, a distinct zone of low separability separates two regions of high discrimination. This screening approach helps in the identification of experimental conditions for model discrimination, but do not relays on the trajectory analysis. Notably, two dynamical systems may reach the same state through different trajectories, a distinction that single-point methods cannot capture.

Figure~\ref{fig:Model_discrimination_index} addresses this limitation by comparing the temporal evolution of discrimination indices derived from cumulative and differentiated yields, with a sampling interval of 30~min. In the low-discrimination scenario (Figure~\ref{fig:Discrimination_index_333}), cumulative yield-based indicators suggest that discrimination is confined to the initial phase. In contrast, rate-based indices display a decline followed by a recovery before ultimately decaying to zero. This behavior occurs when one model initially predicts a lower extract but subsequently surpasses the other, resulting in a crossover of model outputs. Consequently, rate-based indices offer improved discrimination during the later stages of extraction, indicating that the measurement strategy affects both the magnitude and the temporal window of discrimination.

In the high-discrimination case (Figure~\ref{fig:Discrimination_index_666}), all indicators demonstrate rapid initial changes and stabilize at their upper bounds at approximately 150 min. This observation suggests that the experiment duration can be reduced by determining when the indicators reach predefined thresholds.

For the same case, $D_J$, AUC, and KS demonstrate similar temporal patterns, the $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ divergence reveals asymmetric behavior. The substantial difference between $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(L\|P)$ and $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|L)$ suggests that one model generates extreme outcomes that are improbable under the alternative model, as evidenced by the output distributions (see Appendix~\ref{App:distributions}).

It was also noted that the rate-based methods display a transient decline between 350 and 450~min, a feature not observed in the cumulative yield indicators. This is due to model output crossover described above.

The previous analysis accounted for parameter uncertainty while assuming noise-free measurements. The impact of empirical measurement error is now evaluated for the most promising operating condition. Figure~\ref{fig:Discrimination_index_666_with_modelling_error} illustrates the changes in discrimination metrics after incorporating measurement error for the experiment at $T = 30^\circ$C, $P = 200$~bar, and $F = 6.67 \times 10^{-5}$~kg/s, which previously demonstrated strong discrimination power under noise-free conditions. Uncertainties are calculated by combining Monte Carlo samples from the parameter variance-covariance matrix with measurement error sampled from the empirical Gaussian distribution, as detailed in Table~\ref{tab:empirical_sigma}. A notable reduction in all discrimination factors is observed: $D_J$ decreases from $\ln(2)$ to less than 0.3 (approximately 50\%), KS declines from 1 to 0.63, and AUC decreases from 1 to 0.9. According to \citet{Hond2022}, AUC values above 0.8 indicate moderate or good discrimination, which suggests that AUC is the most robust index among those considered. Since AUC quantifies the probability of correct pairwise ranking rather than absolute distributional distance, it is less affected by symmetric measurement noise that shifts both model outputs similarly.

Expanding the set of viable operating conditions for subsequent discrimination experiments is possible by permitting dynamic adjustments to these conditions.
